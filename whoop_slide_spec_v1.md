## SLIDE 7 — Real Options

**Slide title:** *DCF prices the business. It doesn't price the optionality.*

**Thesis line:** WHOOP holds one major optionality bet that the DCF doesn't capture: reimbursable healthcare. We value it as a probability-weighted decision tree, not as a Black-Scholes call.

**Visual — Decision tree (left two-thirds of slide):**

```
                          ┌─ Materializes (P=20%) ──→ Discounted EV: $3.5B
2033 binary gate ────────┤
"Healthcare reimbursable?"└─ Fails / stalls (P=80%) ──→ EV contribution: $0

Expected EV contribution = 0.20 × $3.5B = $0.70B
```

**Right-side panel — How we got there:**

| Input | Value | Anchored to |
|---|---|---|
| 2033 ending members | 8.2M | DCF model |
| × Eligibility (qualifying conditions) | 40% | CDC: ~40% adults have cardiometabolic risk |
| = Eligible members (2033) | 3.3M | |
| × Payer-paid annual ARPU | $300 | Apple/Aetna pilots ($50-300); Dexcom commercial ($4-6K) — WHOOP positioned in middle |
| = Incremental annual revenue | $980M | |
| × Healthcare-anchored multiple | 6.0x | Bucket-3 medtech median |
| = Incremental EV at exercise (undiscounted) | $5.9B | |
| ÷ (1 + 10.84%)^5 (5yr to exercise) | $3.5B | Discount factor; CMS pathway 5yr (CMS Innovation pathway) |
| × P(materializes) | 20% | iRhythm pathway took 7+ years; Dexcom 15+ years; WHOOP has CMS Innovation Center selection but no CPT codes filed |
| **= Expected option value** | **$0.70B** | |

**Methodology callout (TWO callouts):**
> *(1) Why decision tree, not Black-Scholes? Black-Scholes requires a tradable underlying. Healthcare reimbursement isn't tradable. Decision tree EV is the right framing — Lecture 3B Arundel.*
>
> *(2) Why 20% probability? Anchored to iRhythm pathway timing (7+ years FDA → CMS national coverage; 83% drawdown along the way), Dexcom (15+ years), and WHOOP-specific signals (CMS Innovation Center selection April 2026, but zero CPT codes filed and no clinical validation in regulated indications).*

**Footer:** `LECTURE 3B / ARUNDEL — PROBABILITY-WEIGHTED CALL ON UNCERTAIN FUTURE CASH FLOWS. NOT BLACK-SCHOLES BECAUSE UNDERLYING NOT TRADABLE.`

**Speaker notes (~90 sec):**
> "The DCF assumes WHOOP stays a consumer subscription. It doesn't price the healthcare reimbursement bet — and that's a real option WHOOP holds, not just hand-waving optionality.
>
> We frame it as a binary gate decision tree, not Black-Scholes. The Arundel lecture taught us that real options are call options on uncertain future cash flows — but Black-Scholes specifically requires the underlying to be tradable. Healthcare reimbursement payoffs aren't traded. So we use a decision tree, which is the right tool.
>
> The math: if WHOOP becomes reimbursable by 2033, we estimate 8.2 million members times 40 percent eligibility — anchored to CDC data on cardiometabolic risk in adults — gives us 3.3 million eligible members. At $300 per member per year of payer-paid ARPU — Apple Watch / Aetna pilots are $50-300, Dexcom is $4,000-6,000 commercial — WHOOP positioned in the middle is $300. That's $980M incremental annual revenue. At a 6x healthcare-anchored multiple, that's $5.9B incremental EV at exercise. Discounted back five years at our WACC, $3.5B today.
>
> Probability of materialization: 20 percent. Anchored to the iRhythm pathway, which took 7-plus years from FDA clearance to stable national CMS coverage and saw an 83 percent equity drawdown along the way. Dexcom took 15-plus years. WHOOP has the CMS Innovation Center selection from April 2026, but zero CPT codes filed and no clinical validation in regulated indications. So 20 percent base case, with bear-bull range of 15 to 25 percent.
>
> Expected value: 20 percent times $3.5B equals $700M. That's the option premium we layer onto the DCF base."

## SLIDE 8 — Comps

**Slide title:** *The comp range is the analysis.*

**Thesis line:** We don't pick three industry buckets and average them. We pick three theses about what WHOOP becomes, and let the dispersion be the answer.

**Visual — Three thesis brackets, each with EV range (left two-thirds):**

```
THESIS 1 — FAILED PIVOT (Bear scenario)
Anchors: Peloton, Sonos, GoPro (failed sub pivots)
Range: $0.7B – $2.5B EV
                          ▓▓▓
                          
THESIS 2 — SUBSCRIPTION EXECUTED (Base scenario)  
Anchors: Spotify, Roku (mature recurring consumer)
Range: $3.0B – $9.7B EV
                                 ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓
                                 
THESIS 3 — HEALTHCARE PLATFORM (Bull / Optionality)
Anchors: Dexcom, iRhythm, ResMed, Masimo (FDA + CMS)
Range: $3.5B – $8.1B EV
                                  ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓
                                  
                                              Series G EV $9.1B ●
                                              Oura EV $11B ━━━ (parallel ref)
```

**Right-side panel — Why three theses, not three buckets:**

> Industry classification (hardware / sub / health-data) is the WRONG dimension. Two companies in the same SIC code can have wildly different multiples (Peloton vs Spotify both subscription-hardware adjacent, 1.2x vs 4.1x). What matters is shared *economic structure*: revenue quality, margin profile, growth trajectory, capital intensity, customer economics.
>
> Within each thesis, comps share economic structure. Across theses, they don't. So we don't average across them.

**Bottom band — Multiple framework + Oura flip:**

| Comp framework | Note |
|---|---|
| EV/Revenue NTM | Standard; treats all revenue equal regardless of margin |
| EV/Gross Profit NTM | **Better for WHOOP** — controls for margin advantage (78% vs 32-44% in subscription comps) |
| Growth-adjusted scatter | Visual narrative; not a $ output |

> **The Oura flip:** WHOOP at $10.1B trades higher than Oura on EV/Rev (15.5x vs 13x) but **lower** on EV/GP (20x vs 26x). WHOOP's margin advantage flips the comparison. Both are private marks subject to same illiquidity/structuring caveats.

**Methodology callout:**
> *We refuse to produce a single weighted comp number. The composite span is $0.7B-$9.7B. The dispersion IS the answer — it tells the audience that "what does the public market pay for WHOOP" depends on which thesis they buy.*

**Footer:** `THREE THESES, NOT THREE BUCKETS. WITHIN-THESIS COMPS SHARE ECONOMIC STRUCTURE. DISPERSION IS THE ANALYTICAL CONTENT.`

**Speaker notes (~90 sec):**
> "Most IB football fields take three industry buckets and weight them. We don't do that, and the reason matters. Industry classification is the wrong dimension. Peloton and Spotify are both subscription businesses with hardware exposure — same industry — and they trade at 1.2x and 4.1x revenue respectively. Industry doesn't predict multiple. Economic structure does.
>
> So we use three theses about what WHOOP becomes. Thesis 1: WHOOP fails to defend subscription, becomes hardware-dependent — like Peloton, Sonos, GoPro. That bracket implies $700M to $2.5B EV. Thesis 2: WHOOP executes as a premium consumer subscription — like Spotify, Roku. That implies $3 to $9.7 billion EV. Thesis 3: WHOOP becomes a reimbursable health-data platform — like Dexcom, iRhythm, ResMed, Masimo. That implies $3.5 to $8.1 billion.
>
> Within each thesis, the comps share economic structure — recurring revenue mix, margin profile, growth trajectory. Across theses, they don't. So we don't average across them. The composite span is $700M to $9.7B. The dispersion IS the answer.
>
> One specific finding worth surfacing: Oura. It's the only direct comp WHOOP has, and it's private. WHOOP at Series G trades at 15.5x recognized revenue versus Oura at 13x. So WHOOP looks more expensive. But on EV / Gross Profit, WHOOP is at 20x and Oura is at 26x. WHOOP is cheaper. The flip is real — it comes from WHOOP's 78 percent gross margin versus Oura's roughly 50 percent blended. Higher margin means each revenue dollar is worth more, and EV/GP catches that. Both are private marks though, so neither validates the other."

**Speaker notes (~120 sec — this is the novelty slide, give it room):**
> "This slide inverts the question. Instead of asking 'what is WHOOP worth,' we ask: what does $10.1B require WHOOP to actually do? >
> The waterfall: start at $10.1B post-money equity. Subtract $1B in net cash, you get $9.1B implied EV. Compound at our WACC of 10.84% for seven years to get to 2033, you get $20.9B implied 2033 enterprise value. Divide by our subscription-anchored exit multiple of 3.5x, that requires $5.97B of 2033 revenue. Our base case for 2033 is $3.27B. So Series G is implying 82 percent more revenue than our base case.
>
> That's the simple version: 'Series G implies 82% more revenue than we model.' That's a number.
>
> The deeper version is more interesting. With 8.4M members at the implied $5.97B revenue, you need $710 ARPU. Our base ARPU at 2033 is $448. So you need $262 more per member. Where does that come from? About $110 from inflation and tier mix shift over seven years. That leaves $152 unaccounted for. The most plausible source: WHOOP Labs, the blood-test product launched in 2025. At $375 per year per attached member, $152 implies roughly 40 percent attach. Our base case has 18 percent attach by 2033.
>
> So Series G is implicitly asking us to believe that 40 percent of WHOOP members will be consuming Labs by 2033. That's a story you can interrogate. Is that plausible? Maybe. Apple Watch Series 9 ECG attach rates are in the high single digits. Premium consumer subscription cross-sell rates rarely exceed 25 percent in two-product setups. 40 percent would be at the high end of any consumer sub category. It's not impossible, but it's a strong claim. And it's the testable claim that the Series G is making."
