## Company Overview

Peloton Interactive, Inc. is a connected fitness platform combining hardware (bikes, treads, rowers) with a recurring subscription for live and on-demand fitness content. Founded in 2012, IPO in September 2019. Headquartered in New York City.

**Why Peloton is the single most important cautionary comp for WHOOP:** Same subsidized-hardware + subscription business model structure. Peloton's trajectory from pandemic darling ($50B+ peak market cap, Jan 2021) to distressed turnaround (~$2B market cap, April 2026) is the clearest real-world illustration of what happens when a hardware-subscription hybrid loses growth momentum. The bull thesis for WHOOP must specifically address why WHOOP avoids this fate.

**Key timeline:**
- **IPO:** September 2019 at ~$8.1B valuation
- **Peak:** January 2021 — stock hit $171.09 (ATH), market cap ~$50B
- **Collapse:** FY2022 — revenue declined, massive losses, CEO replaced
- **Turnaround:** FY2024-FY2025 — cost cuts, FCF positive, but revenue still declining
- **Current:** April 2026 — $2.07B market cap, still shrinking revenue but profitable on adjusted basis

## WHOOP Comparability Notes

### Why Peloton IS the cautionary comp

1. **Same business model DNA:** Subsidized hardware + recurring subscription revenue. Both companies lose money (or make thin margins) on hardware and depend on subscription economics for profitability.

2. **COVID-era growth distortion:** Both benefited from pandemic-era tailwinds in health/fitness. Peloton's trajectory shows what happens when that tailwind reverses — the growth invested in capacity and content becomes a fixed cost burden.

3. **Churn is destiny:** Peloton's churn roughly doubled from 0.73% to 1.8% post-COVID. If WHOOP's churn follows a similar trajectory (and WHOOP does not disclose churn), the subscription economics deteriorate rapidly.

4. **S&M addiction:** Peloton spent 35-55% of revenue on SG&A to drive/maintain growth. When they cut spending, revenue fell further. This is the "hardware flywheel" in reverse.

5. **Revenue mix shift is a feature AND a bug:** The shift to subscription-dominant revenue improves gross margins but also means the company is increasingly dependent on a shrinking subscriber base paying recurring fees.

### Why the WHOOP bull thesis argues WHOOP avoids Peloton's fate

1. **No manufacturing infrastructure:** WHOOP outsources hardware manufacturing (contract manufacturers), avoiding Peloton's massive Tonic (Precor) acquisition, factory buildout, and logistics infrastructure that created enormous fixed cost leverage in the wrong direction.

2. **Different price point and commitment:** WHOOP's ~$30/month subscription (with "free" device) is lower friction than Peloton's $44/month + $1,400+ bike. Lower commitment = lower churn sensitivity to macro cycles.

3. **Passive vs. active use case:** WHOOP is worn 24/7 passively. Peloton requires active 30-60 minute workout sessions. Passive devices have inherently higher daily engagement and lower churn.

4. **Healthcare optionality:** WHOOP has FDA-cleared ECG, blood pressure, and biomarker capabilities. Peloton has no healthcare pivot. Abbott and Mayo as strategic investors give WHOOP a credible pathway Peloton never had.

5. **No content treadmill:** Peloton must continuously produce expensive fitness content (instructors, studios, production). WHOOP's value is in data analytics and algorithms, which scale with marginal cost near zero.

6. **Unit economics at entry:** WHOOP gives the device free with subscription — the entire revenue stream is recurring from day one. Peloton's hardware revenue was 78% of revenue at peak, creating massive revenue volatility as hardware demand cycles.

7. **Market size trajectory:** Peloton's TAM (home fitness equipment) proved more cyclical than expected. WHOOP's TAM (continuous health monitoring) is expanding as healthcare and wellness converge, supported by regulatory tailwinds (FDA clearances).

### Key structural differences to quantify

| Dimension | Peloton | WHOOP | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hardware COGS as % rev | 50-80% of total rev | <20% [E] | WHOOP has far less COGS volatility |
| Subscription GM | 67-72% | ~75-80% [E] | Similar range, WHOOP may be higher (no content costs) |
| Monthly subscription | $44 | ~$30 | Lower price point = lower cancellation friction |
| Subscriber trajectory | Peaked and declining | Growing 103% YoY | Opposite directions |
| Churn (monthly) | 1.8% (rising) | Not disclosed | **FLAGGED_GAP**: WHOOP churn is THE key unknown |
| FCF generation | $320M+ (FY2025) | Cash flow positive (2025) | Peloton ahead in absolute terms (much larger base) |
| EV/Revenue | 1.16x | 9.2x (Series G implied) | 8x gap — justified only if growth + optionality hold |

### The bear case for WHOOP, Peloton-style

If WHOOP's growth decelerates sharply post-IPO (as Peloton's did post-COVID), if churn increases as the early-adopter cohort matures, and if the healthcare pivot stalls, WHOOP could face:
- Revenue multiple compression from 9x toward 2-4x
- Enterprise value of $2-4B vs. the $10.1B Series G mark
- The same "great product, bad business" narrative that haunted Peloton

**The central question:** Is WHOOP's 103% growth organic and sustainable, or is it Peloton's 120% growth circa FY2021 — a sugar high that precedes a multi-year hangover?
